# Evaluation metrics for ordinal clinical AI triage classification

**A GP inbox urgency classifier demands a multi-metric evaluation framework anchored by per-class sensitivity for safety-critical classes, supplemented by ordinal-aware metrics and robust test set design.** No single metric suffices for this problem—class imbalance, ordinal structure, and asymmetric error costs each require dedicated measurement. No regulator (FDA, TGA, or Medsafe) mandates specific sensitivity thresholds; instead, the closest clinical benchmark is the **≤5% undertriage rate** from trauma triage standards, translating to ≥95% sensitivity for the highest-acuity class. The system almost certainly requires TGA registration as a Class IIa medical device, since AI-enabled clinical decision support software explicitly fails Australia's CDSS exemption criteria.

---

## 1. Metrics that match this problem's structure

Published clinical NLP triage studies overwhelmingly report **ROC-AUC and sensitivity** as primary metrics. A systematic review of 60 ML/NLP studies for emergency department triage found these two metrics were the most commonly used, with only 23.4% of studies achieving ROC-AUC above 0.95 and 12.5% reporting sensitivity above 0.95 (Zaboli A et al., "Improving triage performance in emergency departments using machine learning and natural language processing: a systematic review," *BMC Emergency Medicine*, 2024, DOI: 10.1186/s12873-024-01135-2, PMC11575054). Wang B et al.'s study on primary-to-secondary care referral triage—the closest published analogue to GP inbox classification—reported precision, recall, F1, accuracy, and AUC, achieving AUC 0.90 ("Improving triaging from primary care into secondary care using heterogeneous data-driven hybrid machine learning," *Decision Support Systems*, 2022, DOI: 10.1016/j.dss.2022.113899).

**Per-class sensitivity** is the most directly safety-relevant metric for this system. It answers the question clinicians and regulators care about most: "Of all truly Immediate documents, what proportion did the system flag?" In an assist-only system, a false positive (over-triage) costs the GP a few seconds of review time; a false negative (under-triage) risks a dangerous result being deprioritised. This asymmetry makes sensitivity for high-urgency classes the irreducible safety metric.

**Macro F1** treats all four urgency classes equally regardless of frequency, exposing poor performance on rare Immediate/Urgent classes that weighted F1 would conceal. This is critical: weighted F1 is dominated by the majority class, so a model excellent at classifying "Routine" but terrible at detecting "Immediate" could still report a high weighted F1 score. Gowda et al. ("Macro-Average: Rare Types Are Important Too," 2021) and Harbecke et al. ("Why only Micro-F1? Class Weighting of Measures for Relation Classification," 2022) recommend macro-averaged metrics when every class matters.

**Quadratic weighted kappa (QWK)** is purpose-built for ordinal classification, penalising misclassifications proportionally to the squared distance between predicted and true classes. Classifying "Immediate" as "Information only" (3 levels away) is penalised **9× more** than classifying it as "Urgent" (1 level away). QWK is widely used in clinical grading systems—Araújo et al. achieved QWK 0.71–0.84 for diabetic retinopathy grading ("DR|GRADUATE," *Medical Image Analysis*, 2020, 63:101715), and Fazekas and Kovács compared 37 ordinal metrics, finding weighted kappa variants the most reliable overall ("Weighted kappa measures for ordinal multi-class classification performance," *Applied Soft Computing*, 2023, 134:110020, DOI: 10.1016/j.asoc.2023.110020).

**PR-AUC** outperforms ROC-AUC for imbalanced datasets where rare positive classes are the focus. Saito and Rehmsmeier demonstrated that ROC plots can be visually deceptive when prevalence is low ("The Precision-Recall Plot Is More Informative than the ROC Plot When Evaluating Binary Classifiers on Imbalanced Datasets," *PLOS ONE*, 2015, 10(3):e0118432, PMC4349800). Cook et al. confirmed in critical care settings that models with excellent AUROC (>0.85) can still have PPV below 0.2 at clinically necessary sensitivity thresholds ("Use of the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve to Evaluate Prediction Models of Rare Critical Illness Events," *Critical Care Medicine*, 2025, PMC12133047).

**Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC)** achieves a high score only when all four confusion matrix quadrants perform well, making it robust to class imbalance. Chicco and Jurman showed MCC correctly indicates poor performance when classifiers fail on one class, while accuracy and F1 give misleadingly high scores ("The advantages of the Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) over F1 score and accuracy in binary classification evaluation," *BMC Genomics*, 2020, 21:6, DOI: 10.1186/s12864-019-6413-7, PMC6941312). However, MCC's multi-class extension is less established, and Zhu argued it can deteriorate under severe imbalance ("On the performance of Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC) for imbalanced dataset," *Pattern Recognition Letters*, 2020, 136:81–86).

The recommended metric framework for this system, in priority order:

| Priority | Metric | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| 1 (Safety) | Sensitivity for Immediate class | Directly measures patient safety; target ≥95% |
| 2 (Safety) | Sensitivity for Urgent class | Second safety boundary; target ≥90% |
| 3 (Overall) | Macro F1 | Equal weighting exposes minority class failures |
| 4 (Ordinal) | Quadratic weighted kappa | Penalises large-distance misclassifications |
| 5 (Imbalance) | PR-AUC per high-urgency class | Threshold-independent safety assessment |
| 6 (Supplementary) | Full confusion matrix + MCC | Essential for clinical safety review |

## 2. No regulator mandates a sensitivity floor, but ≥95% is the defensible benchmark

**No regulatory body—FDA, EU MDR, TGA, or Medsafe—has published a specific numeric sensitivity threshold for clinical AI triage systems.** Performance requirements are evaluated case-by-case relative to intended use and risk-benefit profile. The FDA's January 2026 draft guidance on AI-enabled device software functions requires description of performance metrics including sensitivity and specificity, and subgroup analysis, but does not specify minimum acceptable values ("Artificial Intelligence-Enabled Device Software-Function: Lifecycle Management and Marketing Submission Recommendations," FDA Draft Guidance, January 2026). The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) requires robust, reliable performance but sets no numeric floors. The TGA follows a risk-proportionate approach with no mandated thresholds.

The closest clinical benchmark is the **American College of Surgeons Committee on Trauma (ACS-COT)** standard of **<5% undertriage** (equivalent to ≥95% sensitivity for the highest-acuity class), with overtriage rates up to 35–50% considered acceptable to maintain safety (Mohan D et al., "Trauma triage in the emergency departments of nontrauma centers," *J Trauma Acute Care Surg*, 2013, 74(6):1541–1547). This benchmark is widely referenced beyond trauma, reflecting the clinical principle that **effective triage prioritises sensitivity over specificity**.

The FDA-cleared Aidoc CARE Foundation Model (510(k) K252970, January 2026) achieved **mean sensitivity 97%** (up to 98.5%) and mean specificity 98% across 14 acute abdomen CT indications. However, a critical December 2025 study of Aidoc's intracranial hemorrhage detection on **101,944 real-world CT exams** found sensitivity of only **82.2%**—a 14 percentage point gap from the FDA clearance figure of 96.15% (Chavoshi M et al., "Real-world performance evaluation of a commercial deep learning model for intracranial hemorrhage detection," *npj Digital Medicine*, 2026, 9:66, DOI: 10.1038/s41746-025-02244-3). Outpatient sensitivity was even lower at 72.2%. Other FDA-cleared systems show similar patterns: Viz ICH achieves 93–95% sensitivity, Avicenna.AI CINA-CSpine 90.3%, and mlHealth Scaida BrainCT-ICH only 86.7%.

A systematic review of clinical AI triage found **52% of AI triage tools tested in real-world hospital settings showed lower sensitivity than reported in validation studies**, with sensitivity typically dropping 8–15 percentage points after deployment (Abdalhalim et al., "Clinical Impact of Artificial Intelligence-Based Triage Systems in Emergency Departments," *Cureus*, 2025, 17(6):e85667, PMC12241827). **For an assist-only system, validation sensitivity targets should therefore be set 10–15 points above the minimum acceptable deployment threshold** to account for this degradation.

No published sensitivity thresholds exist specifically for GP inbox or document urgency classification—this is an identified research gap. The closest analogue is a pharmacovigilance NLP triage model achieving sensitivity 0.86 for identifying safety reports requiring clinical review (Gosselt HR et al., "Natural language processing for automated triage and prioritization of individual case safety reports," *Front Drug Saf Regul*, 2023, 3:1120135). For this GP system, **≥95% sensitivity for "Immediate" and ≥90% for "Urgent"** are defensible targets based on the convergence of clinical triage benchmarks, FDA-cleared AI device performance, and the principle that the cost of under-triage vastly exceeds over-triage cost.

## 3. Ordinal errors demand partial credit and asymmetric cost weighting

Exact-match accuracy alone is inadequate for ordinal urgency scales. Gaudette and Japkowicz demonstrated that "little attention has been paid as to how to evaluate [ordinal classification] problems, with many authors simply reporting accuracy, which does not account for the severity of the error" ("Evaluation Methods for Ordinal Classification," *Advances in Artificial Intelligence, Canadian AI 2009*, LNCS 5549, DOI: 10.1007/978-3-642-01818-3_25). When a model predicts "Routine" but the true label is "Urgent" (2 ordinal steps apart), this is categorically worse than predicting "Urgent" when the truth is "Immediate" (1 step)—yet exact-match accuracy penalises both identically.

**Three ordinal evaluation approaches are well-supported in the literature.** First, **quadratic weighted kappa** penalises errors by squared ordinal distance. Cohen originally defined weighted kappa with provision for "scaled disagreement or partial credit" (*Psychological Bulletin*, 1968, 70(4):213–220), and de la Torre et al. showed QWK outperforms standard cross-entropy as both a loss function and evaluation metric for ordinal clinical classification ("Weighted kappa loss function for multi-class classification of ordinal data in deep learning," *Pattern Recognition Letters*, 2018, 105:144–150, DOI: 10.1016/j.patrec.2017.05.018). Second, **mean absolute error (MAE)** and its macro-averaged variant (AMAE) directly quantify the average number of ordinal levels a prediction is off. Baccianella et al. proposed AMAE specifically for imbalanced ordinal data ("Evaluation Measures for Ordinal Regression," *Proc. ISDA 2009*, pp. 283–287, DOI: 10.1109/ISDA.2009.230), and Cruz-Ramírez et al. proposed maximum MAE (MMAE) to ensure the worst-performing class is optimised—important for safety-critical minority classes ("Metrics to guide a multi-objective evolutionary algorithm for ordinal classification," *Neurocomputing*, 2014, 135:21–31). Third, **1-off accuracy** (adjacent-class tolerance) measures the proportion of predictions correct or within one ordinal level, implemented in the `dlordinal` Python package (Bérchez-Moreno et al., "dlordinal: a Python package for deep ordinal classification," *Neurocomputing*, 2025, 622:1–19, DOI: 10.1016/j.neucom.2024.129305).

The strong consensus from clinical AI literature is to **use both ordinal metrics and binary collapse simultaneously**. Ordinal metrics (QWK, MAE, 1-off accuracy) evaluate overall classification performance and error severity, while binary collapse (sensitivity/specificity for "Immediate+Urgent" vs. rest) directly evaluates the safety-critical decision boundary. Hicks et al. recommend using "a combination of multiple metrics and interpret the performance holistically" ("On evaluation metrics for medical applications of artificial intelligence," *Scientific Reports*, 2022, 12:5979, PMC8993826). Collapsing ordinal categories to binary loses information and reduces statistical power (Ananth and Kleinbaum, "Regression Models for Ordinal Responses," *Int J Epidemiol*, 1997, 26:1323–1333), but the binary safety boundary is what regulators and clinicians understand most intuitively.

**Asymmetric error costs should be explicitly modelled** because under-triage is categorically more dangerous than over-triage. George, Lu, and Chang proposed a cost-sensitive performance metric specifically for ordinal classification in biomedical applications that accounts for "inherent ordinal data structure, total misclassification cost, and imbalanced class distribution" ("Cost-sensitive Performance Metric for Comparing Multiple Ordinal Classifiers," *Artificial Intelligence Research*, 2016, 5(1):135–143, PMC5217743). A practical cost matrix should assign under-triage costs that escalate quadratically with distance: predicting "Information only" when truth is "Immediate" (cost = 16) versus predicting "Urgent" when truth is "Immediate" (cost = 4), while over-triage costs remain low (cost = 0.5 per level). The PAC+ model for Singapore ED triage implemented a real-world example of asymmetric cost: "finetuning only goes in one direction, up-triaging patients with high mortality risk" (Wong, 2024).

## 4. Test sets need ≥138 positives per class for reliable sensitivity estimates

The **Buderer formula** provides the foundational sample size calculation: n = Z² × Se × (1−Se) / W² / Prevalence, where Z = 1.96 for 95% CI, Se = expected sensitivity, and W = desired confidence interval half-width (Buderer NM, "Statistical methodology: I. Incorporating the prevalence of disease into the sample size calculation for sensitivity and specificity," *Academic Emergency Medicine*, 1996, 3(9):895–900). For a class with 5% prevalence, expected sensitivity of 90%, and desired ±5% CI width, approximately **138 true positive cases** from that class are required—necessitating a total N ≈ 2,760 at natural prevalence. For ±10% CI, approximately 35 true positives suffice. Hajian-Tilaki provides comprehensive tabulated sample sizes for various accuracy levels ("Sample size estimation in diagnostic test studies of biomedical informatics," *J Biomedical Informatics*, 2014, 48:193–204, DOI: 10.1016/j.jbi.2014.02.013).

The test set should **primarily use natural prevalence** but **enrich for rare classes** to achieve adequate per-class sample sizes. This dual approach is well-supported. Varoquaux and Cheplygina distinguish intrinsic metrics (sensitivity, specificity—invariant to prevalence) from extrinsic metrics (PPV, NPV—prevalence-dependent), noting that "metrics that are intrinsic characteristics of the classifier are independent of the situation in which the model is going to be used" ("Evaluating Machine Learning Models and Their Diagnostic Value," *Machine Learning for Brain Disorders*, Springer, 2023, DOI: 10.1007/978-1-0716-3195-9_20). An enriched test set yields valid sensitivity/specificity estimates but **not** valid PPV/NPV; these must be recalculated using Bayes' theorem with known natural prevalence.

**Hidden stratification** is a critical risk. Homeyer et al. documented that AI solutions accurate on aggregate often perform >20% worse on low-prevalence subsets, recommending solutions be "evaluated independently on relevant subsets" ("Recommendations on compiling test datasets for evaluating artificial intelligence solutions in pathology," *Modern Pathology*, 2022, 35:1759–1769, DOI: 10.1038/s41379-022-01147-y, PMC9708586). Holtman et al. identified six study designs suitable for low-prevalence situations, including stratified sampling and enriched designs with inverse probability weighting to recover unbiased estimates ("Development of practical recommendations for diagnostic accuracy studies in low-prevalence situations," *J Clinical Epidemiology*, 2019, 114:38–48, DOI: 10.1016/j.jclinepi.2019.05.018).

A critical recent finding from Roesler et al. shows that class imbalance correction methods (oversampling, SMOTE, undersampling) applied to training data cause significant miscalibration of predictions ("Class imbalance correction in artificial intelligence models leads to miscalibrated clinical predictions," *medRxiv*, 2026, DOI: 10.64898/2026.03.04.26347634). This argues against artificial balancing of training data and cautions that balanced test sets may give misleading impressions of deployment performance.

The practical recommendation: assemble **~1,000–1,500 total items** in a stratified enriched test set targeting ~100–150 items per urgency class, plus a separate **natural-prevalence holdout** of ~300–500 items for calibration and PPV/NPV assessment. Use **bootstrap BCa confidence intervals** (≥2,000 iterations) for per-class metrics on the rare Immediate class, as normal-approximation CIs can produce intervals outside [0,1] with small samples (DiCiccio and Efron, "Bootstrap confidence intervals," *Statistical Science*, 1996, 11(3):189–228).

## 5. Five documented pitfalls that directly threaten inbox triage evaluation

Hicks et al. ("On evaluation metrics for medical applications of artificial intelligence," *Scientific Reports*, 2022, 12:5979, DOI: 10.1038/s41598-022-09954-8, PMC8993826) examined ML studies in gastroenterology and identified five pitfalls by recalculating reported metrics and computing unreported ones. **All five apply directly to GP inbox triage.**

**Pitfall 1 — Precision/recall trade-off masking.** A system reporting 99.7% recall had precision as low as 0.33 when class distribution was extremely imbalanced. For inbox triage, if "Immediate" documents are ~2% of volume, high recall for them could generate many false "Immediate" alerts, creating alert fatigue that undermines the system's clinical value. **Pitfall 2 — Mixture-dependent tuning.** Thresholds tuned on one class distribution fail on another. Inbox urgency distributions shift seasonally (flu season increases "Urgent"), by practice demographics, and over time—a model tuned on one distribution may degrade silently. **Pitfall 3 — Post-hoc data exclusion.** One study removed 15% of test cases where the model wasn't confident, inflating reported performance. For triage, excluding ambiguous documents—precisely those where AI assistance matters most—would severely misrepresent real-world utility. **Pitfall 4 — Separated class evaluation hiding overall failure.** A system reporting high sensitivity (0.94) and specificity (0.96) separately had MCC near chance (−0.05), revealing it was biased toward one class. **Pitfall 5 — NPV oversight.** Reporting PPV without NPV conceals asymmetric errors; high PPV with low NPV means the system misses more true positives than it generates false alarms.

**Spectrum bias** is the most dangerous methodological threat for this use case. Tseng et al. experimentally demonstrated that an AI model trained on extreme-spectrum data (severe vs. no disease) achieved AUC 0.91 but degraded to AUC 0.87 on whole-spectrum data, with specificity dropping from 84% to 73% ("Spectrum bias in algorithms derived by artificial intelligence," *European Heart Journal - Digital Health*, 2021, 2(4):561–567, PMC9707965). For inbox triage, evaluating on clear-cut documents—obviously urgent cancer results alongside routine appointment reminders—will inflate performance. The clinical value lies in correctly triaging the ambiguous borderline cases.

**Shortcut learning** poses specific risks for NLP document classification. Geirhos et al. defined shortcuts as "decision rules that perform well on standard benchmarks but fail to transfer to real-world scenarios" ("Shortcut learning in deep neural networks," *Nature Machine Intelligence*, 2020, 2:665–673). For GP inbox triage, the model may learn to classify based on document source or template (oncology referrals → Urgent) rather than clinical content, or exploit formatting cues (bolded headers, "URGENT" stamps) that won't generalise across healthcare systems. Zech et al. demonstrated a pneumonia CNN could identify which hospital system a radiograph came from with 99.95% accuracy and leveraged this as a diagnostic shortcut ("Variable generalization performance of a deep learning model to detect pneumonia in chest radiographs," *PLoS Medicine*, 2018, 15(11):e1002683, PMC6219764).

Wynants et al. reviewed 232 COVID-19 prediction models and rated **all at high risk of bias**, with common failures including non-representative controls, poor calibration assessment, and high overfitting risk ("Prediction models for diagnosis and prognosis of covid-19: systematic review and critical appraisal," *BMJ*, 2020, 369:m1328, PMC7222643). Nagendran et al. found 72% of 81 clinical AI studies were at high risk of bias, yet 75% claimed AI was comparable or better than clinicians ("Artificial intelligence versus clinicians," *BMJ*, 2020, 368:m689, PMC7190037). The TRIPOD+AI statement provides 27 reporting items for clinical prediction models using ML, including requirements for subgroup evaluation, calibration assessment, and algorithmic bias analysis (Collins GS et al., *BMJ*, 2024, 385:e078378, PMC11019967). The STARD-AI reporting guideline adds 18 new items beyond STARD 2015, including dataset representativeness and performance error analysis (Sounderajah V et al., *Nature Medicine*, 2025, 31:3283–3289).

## 6. TGA requires ARTG registration; New Zealand's framework is in flux

**This product is almost certainly a regulated medical device under TGA.** An AI system processing patient-specific clinical data to suggest urgency categories falls within the medical device definition under the Therapeutic Goods Act 1989, Section 41BD. Critically, the TGA has **explicitly stated that AI-enabled CDSS cannot qualify for the exemption**: "With the rapid uptake of artificial intelligence and its growing incorporation into medical devices, it is important to understand that an AI-enabled CDSS will not meet the exemption criteria" (TGA, "Understanding clinical decision support software," updated October 2025, https://www.tga.gov.au/resources/guidance/understanding-clinical-decision-support-software). The TGA considers AI/ML systems to be "black box" systems where the health professional cannot independently verify the basis for recommendations—failing the transparency criterion required for exemption (TGA, "Exemption for Certain Clinical Decision Support Software," August 2022, https://www.tga.gov.au/sites/default/files/2022-08/exemption-for-certain-clinical-decision-support-software.pdf).

**The most likely classification is Class IIa** under TGA's classification rules (Schedule 2, Part 4, Rule 4.5 of the Therapeutic Goods (Medical Devices) Regulations 2002). The software provides information to a GP (not making autonomous decisions), so the "one class lower" step-down rule applies. However, because the system triages across all clinical documents including potentially life-threatening conditions, TGA may classify as **Class IIb** depending on how it interprets the highest-risk scenario. TGA has shown willingness to up-classify SaMD compared to the EU (TGA, "Classification of active medical devices including software-based medical devices," October 2021, https://www.tga.gov.au/sites/default/files/classification-of-active-medical-devices-including-software-based-medical-devices.pdf). The regulatory pathway requires conformity assessment certification (from TGA or an EU Notified Body), ISO 13485 quality management system, and ARTG inclusion before supply.

The TGA does **not prescribe specific sensitivity thresholds or metrics** but requires AI-specific evidence including: algorithm and model design description; statistically justified dataset sizes; Australian population representativeness; bias assessment; overfitting analysis; data drift monitoring; and performance metrics across subgroups. The TGA explicitly follows the **IMDRF Good Machine Learning Practice (GMLP) 10 Guiding Principles**, including that test datasets must be representative and independent of training data, and testing must demonstrate performance under clinically relevant conditions (TGA, "Evidence requirements for software using AI," https://www.tga.gov.au/products/medical-devices/software-and-artificial-intelligence/manufacturing/artificial-intelligence-ai-and-medical-device-software/evidence-requirements-software-using-ai). Key standards referenced include IEC 62304 (software lifecycle), ISO 14971 (risk management), and IEC 62366-1 (usability).

The TGA's approach is **more restrictive than the FDA's** regarding AI CDS: TGA provides a blanket statement that AI-enabled CDSS will not meet exemption criteria, whereas FDA evaluates function-by-function using four statutory criteria under the 21st Century Cures Act. Both regulators require that CDS software must enable clinicians to independently review recommendation bases; both treat AI/ML-based CDS as requiring regulation. The FDA's January 2026 final guidance clarified that CDS for time-critical decisions generally does not qualify as non-device CDS (FDA, "Clinical Decision Support Software: Guidance for Industry and FDA Staff," January 2026).

**New Zealand presents a regulatory gap.** Under the current Medicines Act 1981, medical devices require only a WAND (Web Assisted Notification of Devices) database listing—a notification process with **no pre-market assessment** of safety, quality, or performance. The Therapeutic Products Act 2023, which would have introduced modern SaMD regulation including pre-market authorisation, was **repealed in 2024** by the coalition government. A replacement Medical Products Bill is in development, with Cabinet agreeing to regulatory settings in July 2025, but decisions on SaMD/AI have not been finalised (NZ Ministry of Health, "Documents on the Medical Products Bill," https://www.health.govt.nz/regulation-legislation/medicines-legislation/regulating-medicines-medical-devices-and-natural-health-products/documents-on-the-medical-products-bill). Under the Trans-Tasman Mutual Recognition Arrangement (TTMRA), **TGA approval would facilitate NZ market entry**. The pragmatic recommendation is to build to TGA standards as the primary regulatory pathway.

## Conclusion

This evaluation framework rests on three pillars that the literature consistently identifies as essential for safety-critical ordinal clinical AI. First, **per-class sensitivity with predefined thresholds** (≥95% for Immediate, ≥90% for Urgent) is the non-negotiable safety metric, supported by the ACS-COT <5% undertriage standard and the sobering evidence that real-world AI triage sensitivity degrades 8–15 points below validation performance. Second, **ordinal-aware metrics (QWK, MAE) combined with binary collapse metrics** capture both the graded severity of errors and the binary safety boundary that clinicians and regulators understand. Third, **enriched test sets with ≥138 true positives per class** and bootstrap confidence intervals are needed to produce statistically reliable estimates for rare high-urgency classes, while a natural-prevalence holdout validates calibration and real-world predictive values. The absence of inbox-specific triage AI benchmarks represents a genuine gap in the literature—this system would be among the first to establish performance standards for the task. Building to TGA Class IIa standards from the outset satisfies Australia's regulatory requirements (including mandatory AI-specific evidence per IMDRF GMLP principles), positions for NZ market entry under TTMRA, and creates an evidence package aligned with emerging international expectations for AI medical device software.
