# Fellowship Assessment Visit — Practical Guide

Source: RNZCGP Standards and Guidelines for Fellowship Assessment (July 2023) + Fellowship Pathway Regulations (Jan 2025) + peer-reported firsthand tips.

---

## Structure of the Day (4–5 hours total)

| Phase | Time |
|-------|------|
| Introduction | up to 30 min |
| Observed consultations (first block) | ~90–100 min |
| Break — records review + discussion | up to 30 min |
| Observed consultations (second block, if needed) | 30–60 min |
| Discussion with candidate | up to 60 min |
| Equipment/premises check | up to 30 min |

Order is agreed between you and assessor before or on the day.

---

## Consultations — What to Know

**How many:** Book 6–8 for the first session + 2–4 as a buffer. The assessor observes up to 8 total.

**Length:** Minimum 10 minutes each. Complex cases with examination should be 15–20 minutes. Do not compress sessions to fit more in.

**Must include:** At least one long-term care consultation AND at least one acute consultation.

**CRITICAL — Do NOT pre-select patients.** The official guidance explicitly states: "Do not set up specific patients. This is usually obvious, does not provide assessors with what the visit must achieve, and may lead to a second visit being necessary (at your expense)."

**What you CAN do:** Ask reception to book your normal mix and ensure the schedule reflects your real caseload. If your practice sees diverse demographics, don't let the schedule be accidentally all routine-adult. Brief reception to book normally.

**If a patient declines the observer:** The assessor uses that time for records review or staff interviews. No penalty to you.

**No feedback between consultations.** The assessor will not comment until the final discussion session.

---

## The Scoring Rubric (Appendix B) — Five Domains, 0–5 Scale

Every consultation is scored across five domains:

| Domain | What's assessed |
|--------|----------------|
| 1. Therapeutic relationship | Rapport, cultural safety, Hui Process, patient-centredness |
| 2. Defining problems | Presenting + associated problems, cultural info gathering, preventive care |
| 3. Examination | Targeted, consent, chaperone offered, culturally safe |
| 4. Management | Safe plan, patient agreement, Māori health models incorporated |
| 5. Clinical thinking | Reasoning, organisation, ethics, health equity |

**Score 3 = pass threshold** for each domain. Score 0 in any domain is dangerous practice.

Cultural competency language appears at EVERY score level in EVERY domain — it is not a separate box to tick, it runs through everything.

## Māori Patients and Cultural Competency — The Real Picture

**Peer tip:** "Failing to see a Māori patient can fail you."
**Official reality:** There is no rule that you must see a Māori patient. However:

- Cultural competency is scored in all 5 domains on a 0–5 scale
- Score 3 (pass) requires: "demonstrates culturally safe consultation and attempts to integrate aspects of a Māori model of health"
- Score 5 requires: "Hui Process incorporated naturally into the consultation"
- The discussion session will probe your Meihana Model and Te Tiriti knowledge even if no Māori patient was seen

**Practical implication:** If your session has no Māori patients, you lose a concrete demonstration opportunity. The assessor can still probe in discussion — but you want at least one real consultation to show it naturally. Ask reception to ensure the day reflects your actual patient population. If you genuinely see Māori patients in your practice, they will likely appear naturally.

**What assessors are watching for:**
- Whakawhānaungatanga (relationship-building) at the opening of every consultation
- Using the Hui Process framework: Mihimihi → Whakawhānaungatanga → Kaupapa → Whakakapi
- Applying the Meihana Model (Te Ao Mārama domains) where relevant
- Asking about whānau, community, cultural identity in an integrated way — not tokenistically
- Addressing health inequity explicitly in management (e.g., referral access, cost, transport)

**Score 0 trigger (automatic concern):** Evidence of racism, bias, or culturally unsafe practice in any consultation.

## Physical Examinations — Yes, Expose and Examine Properly

**Peer tip:** "Do proper examination, must expose the area — like a real OSCE."
**Official confirmation:** This is accurate.

The scoring rubric for Domain 3 states:
- **Score 0:** No examination / patient consent not sought / culturally or medically unsafe
- **Score 3:** Examination limited but appropriate for major problems, consent obtained
- **Score 4:** Appropriate and adequate for most problems; specific consent for sensitive exams; chaperone offered
- **Score 5:** Considerate, thorough, culturally competent; chaperone offered; variations negotiated

**What this means in practice:**
- Always explicitly ask for consent before examining ("Is it okay if I examine your [area]?")
- Expose the body part being examined — don't examine through clothing
- Offer a chaperone for any intimate or sensitive examination (and document it)
- Make sure the examination couch area is private (patient not visible if door opens)
- A chaperone notice must be visible near the examination couch
- Document your examination findings before the assessor leaves the premises

## AI Scribe / Voice-to-Text — Unresolved, Ask First

The July 2023 official standards do not mention AI scribes or dictation tools (the document predates widespread adoption).

The only relevant note: records must not use "auto texting to the detriment of veracity."

**You must ask RNZCGP directly before the visit.** Contact: gpep2@rnzcgp.org.nz

Ask: "Is use of an AI scribe or voice-to-text dictation tool permitted during Fellowship Assessment Visit consultations?"

Do not assume it is allowed. Do not assume it is banned. Get it in writing.

---

## Notes — Must Be Completed on the Day

The assessor will request a copy of notes for all patients seen before leaving the premises. You must complete your write-up while the assessor is still there.

This means: your note-writing must be fast and during/immediately after each consultation. If you rely on catch-up at end of day, this is a problem.

---

## The Discussion Session (up to 60 min)

The assessor will probe across several areas. Prepare for all of these:

**Clinical:**
- Walk through cases seen and your clinical reasoning
- Hypothetical "What if..." questions (e.g., "What if the patient had been a child? On warfarin? Māori?")
- Show a referral letter you are proud of
- Reference resources you use (UpToDate, BPAC, etc.)

**Practice systems:**
- Emergency drill — who does what, do you rehearse it?
- Cold chain compliance
- Controlled drug storage and records
- Sterilisation procedures
- Significant events register — what's in it, how do you review it?
- Complaints procedure — what are the timelines?
- Safe transfer of care (e.g., end of day results inbox)
- After-hours arrangements

**Professional:**
- How do you keep your knowledge current? (peer group, CME, reading)
- Self-care, work-life balance, stress management, resilience
- Your own GP registration (are you registered with a GP who is not yourself?)
- Not treating family members
- Community services you use/refer to

**Cultural:**
- How do you apply the Hui Process in practice?
- How do you address barriers to healthcare access for Māori?
- Your understanding of Te Tiriti obligations in general practice

## Outcome and Timeline

**Three possible outcomes:**
1. All indicators met → Fellowship granted
2. Minor gaps → given time and conditions to remedy (no repeat visit)
3. Major deficiencies → full repeat visit required at your expense, with conditions

The assessor writes a report. Two censors review it, then the Censor in Chief makes the final decision. **Do not contact the assessor about the outcome.**

Notification takes **up to 8 weeks**.

The Censor in Chief's decision is final. Only a process appeal is possible (not an outcome appeal).

**18-month clock:** All other Fellowship requirements (COPS, College membership) must be satisfied within 18 months of a successful visit, or another visit is required.

## Week Before — Action Checklist

**Admin:**
- Submit application form + clinical record review + CV to College
- Print two copies of Declaration of Confidentiality for assessor to sign
- Print patient consent forms (one per patient, consent to observer)
- Inform all staff what the visit is and why it matters
- Arrange refreshments for assessor

**Room:**
- Extra chair for assessor (not between doctor and patient; positioned unobtrusively)
- Chaperone notice visible near examination couch
- Screensaver/password lock on all terminals
- H&DC rights poster in waiting room
- Consultation cost notices visible

**Equipment:**
- Check every item on the equipment checklist against expiry dates and calibration records
- Glucometer: confirm calibration number matches strip
- Sphygmomanometer: calibration within last year (if aneroid)
- Emergency drugs: check expiry dates, quantity, refrigeration where required

**Systems knowledge:**
- Know the emergency drill without hesitation
- Know where the significant events register is and what's in it
- Know the complaints procedure and exact timeframes
- Know cold chain documentation location

**Clinical preparation:**
- Revise Hui Process and Meihana Model until natural, not rehearsed-sounding
- Run through BPAC NZ guidelines for common long-term conditions
- Prepare a referral letter you'd be proud to show
- Think through your peer learning / CME activities — be specific

**Booking:**
- 6–8 consultations first session, 2–4 buffer
- Normal booking — not pre-selected patients
- Minimum 10 min per slot; 15–20 min for complex/examination cases
- Must include at least one long-term care and one acute
